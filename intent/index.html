The Thesis

The bottleneck of AI isn't answers.
It's intent.

Everyone is racing to build better answers. The harder, more valuable problem is upstream — knowing what a person actually wants, the moment before they've finished saying it. That's the layer I'm building.

Live demo▶ Feel it — it finishes your thought before you do
01 / The structural bet

The model layer is commoditizing — frontier capability is becoming a utility you rent by the token. The app layer is a wrapper race, where a feature gets copied in a weekend. Durable advantage lives in neither.

The one surface almost no one is building is the translation itself: the gap between a fuzzy human want and a precise, machine-actionable intent. Today we cross that gap by hand — we've all quietly learned to "write good prompts." That's the human contorting to fit the machine. It's backwards, and it won't hold.

Every assistant on the market lives downstream of an intent you've already half-formed in your head. Almost no product is paying attention to the moment that intent is forming. That moment sits upstream of everything that follows — and upstream is where the leverage is.

So I'm not building a feature. I'm building the layer between every human and every machine that thinks.

What the search box was to the web, the intent layer is to AI: the default, almost involuntary first step. Whoever owns the moment intent forms gets to route everything that comes after it. That's the prize — and I think it's the biggest one on the board.

02 / The wager, made concrete

A thesis this big is easy to say and worthless without a concrete wager. Here's mine: the first build is a Chrome extension — chosen deliberately over a native input method.

Why: the DOM exposes far richer semantic context than an OS-level IME can ever see. An input method only knows you're typing; the page knows what you're typing into, about what, and why. I'll pay the cost of platform constraint to buy that depth — because at this stage, depth of context, not breadth of surface, is what makes reading intent actually work.

At the core is an intent-classification system — the part that turns captured context into something the rest of the stack can act on. That's the piece I own.

03 / Where it stands

A Delaware C-Corp with three engineering co-founders, moving from research-project mode into a real operating cadence. I'd rather tell you exactly what stage this is at than dress it up — knowing the difference between those two modes is most of the job.
